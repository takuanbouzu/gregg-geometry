One · The sequence

The Fleishman Sequence

A new root-triangle sequence — the spiral of square roots, carried into three dimensions.

Begin with a unit right triangle. Its hypotenuse is √2. Raise a unit leg from that hypotenuse and the new hypotenuse is √3 — and so on, winding outward forever.

Each turn is a whole-number area, each edge an irrational length. The triangle with legs of 1 and √2 and hypotenuse √3 is the one that matters most.

Two · The triangle

It fits where nothing else does.

Between the right angle of the square and the sixty degrees of the equilateral triangle there is a gap — and exactly one triangle that fills it. Its acute angle is 55°.

The Lost Triangle · in color
The square, the √2 fold, and the √3 hypotenuse
Lost Triangle template · 1 : √2 : √3
Construction radii R=1, √2, √3 · angles 55° and 35°

Found inside a unit cube, the triangle is read straight off the solid: an edge is 1, a face diagonal is √2, and the corner-to-corner space diagonal is √3 — meeting at a single right angle.

A note on the angles: written here as 35° and 55°, they are exactly arctan(1/√2) ≈ 35.264° and arctan √2 ≈ 54.736° — irrational, because they fall straight out of the √2 : 1 ratio. Rounded in the prose; shown to full precision in the interactive lessons.

+ Theory note · symmetry — a supporting lens

Stand back from the drawing and the Lost Triangle behaves less like a fixed figure than a generative seed: the same proportions, carried three ways.

Reflection
Mirror it across an edge and the proportions hold — the move that folds the triangle into the rhombic dodecahedron. (the bronze plate above is a reflection study.)
Rotation
Turn it about an axis and it finds new alignments under the same rule — the plan that stands up into space.
Translation
Repeat it step by step and it propagates — one module becoming the whole of space.

Reflection, rotation and translation are standard terms from geometry; here they only name what Gregg's constructions already do.
